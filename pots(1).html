Lorraine Steigner | Fall 2023¶

Archaeologists must process and cross reference a massive amount of data to get an accurate idea of historical trends and activities through material evidence-- the more data the better, because it allows for more solid conclusions to be drawn in a field that must constantly adjust its conclusions in the face of new data. Data analytics can help identify relevant trends-- especially across cultural specialites-- that can guide archaeologists and classicists in the

For my project I want to work on processing data on vase and pot artifacts in the Mediterranean (especially Greece and Rome), which are signiifgant for how they reflect both idea exchange-- as styles spread across regions-- and also the particular culture of the people creating them, since they are an art form. I'm particularly interested in what the intersection of time and location data of these artifacts may tell us about trade patterns and migration routes of ancient peoples of whom we have no or little written record of said trade patterns and migrations, and I've conducted some very basic analysis of that here.

The primary dataset used is the Beazley Archive Pottery Database (BAPD), which contains 132,861 vases from various sites around Europe and the Mediterranean, with data on location, style, and estimated dating. The BAPD is the most extensive, accessible, and well-documented vase dataset I could find, and is particularly useful for its inclusion of other datasets in its documentation of the vases, which will be invaluable in further stages when I plan to cross refernece

Through analysis of the BAPD and other datasets I aim to come to conclusions about the travel, trade patterns, and cultural exchange of the ancient world in a much more efficient way than I could by processing that data by hand... and in this way become more connected with the ancient cultures who left them behind for us to look at today.

Reformatting Existing Data¶

I've done the easy part of just filtering out the data I want to look at and now I will reorganise the columns I have kept to be more easily readable.

In particular, I want to:

  • split Date into two columns-- the early estimate and the late estimate.
  • split provenance into three columns -- country, region, and site.

DATES

Since all the dates for these artifacts are estimates, I will separate the Date column into two columns-- the upper estimate for when the piece was made and the lower estimate for when the piece was made.

There are two anomalies in the date formatting, and both deal with only one date being given. In both cases I simply duplicate the date that is given-- this means the earliest date and the latest date will be identical for now.

Then I just split the Date string into the earlier and later dates and add to their respective columns. I delete the original date column when I'm done.

LOCATION

I skimmed through all the unique values in the provenance table to understand the different formats the locations were given in. All to some extent were represented as a comma separated list, starting with the broadest region (a country almost every time) and then moving down to sub-region/province/etc, and often having the specific site the artifact was found in listed as well. Some instances only had the country and some had a long list getting to a very specific site.

I decided to just go through the comma separated list and add them to the country-> region -> site -> highly specific site columns in order, since they were fairly consistent. Also, since right now I'm only worried about the broadest country category, I wasn't as concerned about cleaning up the region-site columns--and because I know I will have to address region and site in further depth when I cross-reference site location with other databases in the future.

This quick analysis reflects that Black-figure pottery had a longer presence in the mediterranean than red-figure pottery, and that their rise and fall in popularity happened concurrently in both Italy and Greece, which is a reflection of a robust trading system between the two neighboring countries, even before the Roman Empire gained a strong presence on the Italian peninsula.

This makes sense-- Greece pionneered both the black-figure and red-figure styles of pottery, so it is likely that the Italian potteries are a reflection on what was being produced in the Greek world. Native Italian potteries, like Etruscan bucchero, would follow different trends. But this dataset as is right now is still only rudimentarily processed, and there's actually no record of any pottery with bucchero technique in the italian dataset I'm using right now-- and all bucchero pottery is italian by definiton.
